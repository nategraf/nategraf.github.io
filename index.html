Checkers for One

Computerized checkers board

For board game lovers, playing checkers on the computer lacks a certain essence to it. Because of this my teammates, Di and Allison, and I decided to take features from computer checkers and bring them to a physical board. Given the two month deadline we decided to target two features in particular, move validation and an artificially intelligent opponent

To display information to the user we decided to put a multicolor LED under each square using LED light strips, and to detect the presence of the pieces using magnetic reed switches with a software backing to translate this information into a game state. I custom designed and built the enclosure, modeling it in Autodesk Inventor and manufacturing it using a CNC programed Shopbot mill. Inside the box there are two plates. The upper plate to hold the led strips, which are visible through the clear top. The lower plate rests 0.8" below the first and holds the delicate reed switches snugly in place at the correct depth the detect accurately. These sensors and lights then pass through two multiplexing boards which I built and into a Rasberry PI 3.0 on which resides the hardware driver, game logic, and artificial intelligence. The AI and potential move calculations are made possible by Harrison Zhao's Checkers C++, while the drivers were written by Di and game logic by Allison.

Parambulator

Based on the orbital guidance tool from Seven Eves of the same name, the Parambulator is a simulation of a spacecraft, called Izzy, in orbit allowing the player to navigate through an asteroid field around earth. Following in tow are a number of small spacecraft called Arklets which follow a form of flocking behavior, with modification to facilitate orbital dynamics. Each Arklet seeks to stay close to Izzy while avoiding adtroids and being sucked into Earth's gravity.

Hack DFW 2015 - RuffPatch Intel Winner

Repairing 18-wheeler trucks is expensive with impacts to shipment reliability, so minimizing the amount of repairs across a fleet of trucks is a chief concern. With this problem in mind my team and I built RuffPatch, an onboard vibration and noise sensor designed to map out road quality and allow fleet managers to choose routes which have low impact on the trucks.
